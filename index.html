01 Spec Fintech Explainer ~820 words

Why Sending $200 from London to Lagos Still Costs More Than It Should

The average cost of sending money across borders sits at 6.4% globally. For the people who rely on remittances most, the cost is often higher. Here is why, and what open payment infrastructure is actually trying to fix.

In 2022, migrants around the world sent $794 billion in remittances to their home countries. That figure, from the World Bank, represents money that low and middle-income households used for food, school fees, medical care, and small business investment. It is also a figure that came after fees. The $794 billion is what arrived. The amount sent was significantly higher.

The World Bank's Remittance Prices Worldwide database tracked the average cost of sending $200 internationally at 6.35% in the first quarter of 2023. For remittance corridors from sub-Saharan Africa, the average was 7.8%. For some corridors, the figure exceeds 10%. The G20 set a target in 2011 of reducing the global average to 5% by 2014. It is now 2024. The target has not been met.

The reason the price stays high is not technical. It is structural.

What happens between the send and the receive

When you send money from London to Lagos, the transaction does not travel directly from your account to the recipient's. It moves through a chain of intermediaries: your bank, one or more correspondent banks, a local settlement institution, and the recipient's bank or mobile wallet provider. Each node in that chain charges a fee for the service of passing the transaction along and takes a margin on the exchange rate. By the time the money arrives, the total cost of the journey has been absorbed from the principal.

The reason this chain exists is that financial systems were built in national silos. Every country has its own banking infrastructure, its own currency, its own regulatory framework, and its own settlement systems. These systems do not natively communicate with each other. The correspondent banking network was the solution developed before the internet, and it has persisted partly because replacing it requires coordination across thousands of institutions in hundreds of jurisdictions, and partly because the institutions that profit from it have limited incentive to see it replaced.

The cost of sending money internationally stays high not because the technology is hard. It is because the infrastructure was built to keep institutions in the middle of every transaction.

What the Interledger Protocol is actually proposing

The Interledger Protocol (ILP) approaches this the way the internet approached incompatible computer networks in the 1970s. TCP/IP did not replace every computer network in existence. It created a common language that allowed those networks to communicate across their differences, so that a message sent from one network could arrive intact on a completely different system without either network needing to be rebuilt.

ILP proposes the same architecture for value transfer. Rather than replacing the banking systems, mobile wallets, and payment networks that exist, it creates a standard that lets those systems transact across their existing infrastructure without routing through a chain of correspondent intermediaries. A mobile money wallet in Nigeria and a digital bank account in the United Kingdom would not need to share a network, a currency, or a corporate relationship to transact directly. They would need to speak ILP.

The practical implication for the person sending $200 to a family member is that a direct transaction between two compliant endpoints removes the correspondent banking chain and its associated fees. The exchange rate still applies. The friction of finding compatible endpoints still applies. But the per-transaction cost structure is fundamentally different when four intermediaries become zero.

Why this has not already happened

Open standards are adopted when the coordination costs of adoption are lower than the benefits they unlock. For internet protocols, adoption accelerated once enough institutions were connected that network effects kicked in: a standard that let you communicate with a hundred institutions was more valuable than the proprietary system that kept you siloed.

For payment infrastructure, the adoption problem is harder. Financial institutions operate under regulatory frameworks that require certainty about who their counterparties are, what laws apply, and how disputes are resolved. An open protocol for value transfer has to provide answers to those questions before regulated institutions can use it, regardless of how technically sound the protocol itself is.

This is the work that is actually difficult: not building the protocol, but building the trust, regulatory acceptance, and institutional adoption that makes the protocol useful at scale. It is social and political infrastructure as much as technical infrastructure. And it is the reason that an organisation devoted to fostering that adoption, through grants, education, policy engagement, and developer support, exists alongside the technical standard itself.

The $794 billion that arrived in 2022 represents the fraction of the global remittance flow that survived the intermediary chain. The Interledger Foundation's work is about changing the conditions under which that chain is even necessary.

Sources: World Bank Remittance Prices Worldwide Database (Q1 2023); World Bank Migration and Development Brief (2023); Interledger Foundation technical documentation; G20 Financial Inclusion Action Plan targets.
